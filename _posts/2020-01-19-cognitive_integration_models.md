Computational Models of Consciousness and Cognitive Integration

Hossein Adeli

Stony Brook University

Abstract

An astonishing feature of the brain is its capability of massive cognitive integration. The brain has the potential to draw on information from any experienced domain to bear upon a novel situation and produce an adaptive response. This orchestrated coherent response in cognitively demanding tasks, is almost always accompanied by conscious awareness. While biologically plausible theories of consciousness have been developed in the past few decades the role they might play in working cognitive models has been largely unexplored. This review paper documents the main tenets of those theories with underlying behavioral and brain imaging evidences and points to the potential implications of those theories in building cognitively capable models. Also discussed is an interpretation of findings of visual search literate in the proposed framework.

Keywords: *consciousness, integration, dynamic core, global workspace, visual search*

Computational Models of Consciousness and Cognitive Integration

There has been a great deal of progress in models of consciousness in the past few decades mainly concerned with perceptual integrations and how that can constitute consciousness versus unconscious content. The main goal of the current review paper is to suggest how theories of consciousness grounded in a general biological theory of the brain can inform and constrain our way of designing and building cognitive systems. A role that has not yet been fully explored and in fact there has been very little place for phenomenal concepts such as imagination, consciousness or affects in intelligent systems.

It is important to make a distinction here between the so called “primary consciousness” and “higher-order consciousness” (Edelman, 1989). Primary consciousness is what, for example, a dog or a macaque monkey might have in addition to humans. It mainly refers to sensory consciousness; having an awareness of the present and the immediate past sensory inputs. Higher order consciousness refers to being conscious of being conscious. It involves a sense of self, most likely depends on language acquisition, and appears to be unique to humans. In this paper, I will use the term consciousness to solely refer to primary consciousness. In that sense, every conscious scene is directed, it is about something, constituting the “consciousness content”, and lasts for about 100 to 200 ms (Tononi, 2012). Consciousness content can be thought of as the focus of attention in the conscious scene (Izhikevich, 2006). Attention can be either toward an external object, token or sensory input or internal memory or mood (I just went from being aware of the pressure of the chair on my back, to the sound of air conditioner, to a feeling of thirst). In that sense, attention is the property of any conscious scene emerging from the competitive bottleneck. Take this view of attention in conscious scene as an assumption as I cannot give much evidences since the claim involves subjective experience, but one can pose this challenge to the reader: have you ever had a conscious experience within which you were not attending to something? That was not about an external token or internal state or memory? Another way to think about this is that when in a laboratory settings, subjects are instructed to attend to the location or feature of an external stimulus, they basically facilitate the access of that external feature or location to the conscious awareness (Baars, 2013). One could be sure that humans do have this primary consciousness because they can report their awareness but this could only be inferred from non-humans behavior.

It is also important to note that I will use the term “action” in this paper to refer to both physical and mental actions (Engel et al., 2013). Physical actions are observable movements of muscle and mental actions involve tasks of recall, imagination and mental arithmetic. Count from 10 down to 1 in your head or imagine how you would feel if you bang your hand on the table; you are performing actions because you are simulating an action in your head and predicting the potential outcome (Hesslow, 2002).

Consider these hypothetical scenarios:

Scenario 1. As I am working on this paper, I decided to copy a paragraph to the next page, I selected the paragraph and pressed ‘Ctrl+C’ then scrolled down to the next page and noticed that a figure caption was misplaced and thought I should copy that but as I decide to do the second copying, I became aware that I would lose what I have in the copy buffer if I do this right now.

This scenario highlights that living organisms do not just passively react to sensory stimuli. Alternatively, they act to satisfy a nested hierarchy of goals within which they actively attend to relevant sensory input. When having a goal in mind, the relevant dimensions of the sensory input will be attended to and if an action will lead to a negative consequence, it will raise to conscious awareness automatically (Gayet, et al., 2015). It is as if the outcome of my intended action and the value associated with it became available to me (I became aware of it because of the negatively valued outcome) immediately after I intend to do it and before I actually do it, the underlying processing of which remained unconscious.

Scenario 2. Imagine a prey animal in the jungle. As he is eating grass, he hears a sound, sees a bush moving and smells something. Few seconds later, a tiger shows up and he runs away. Next time, when he hears a sound, sees a bush moving and smells something familiar, the animal runs away immediately, without seeing the tiger\!

So why did the animal run away? Because when the three different sensory inputs which are not individually associated with danger, happened simultaneously, the coalitions of these brain processes, each a discrimination within the specialized modality, formed a coalition, came to awareness and manifested an adaptive behavior in this case, escaping. Unlike a laboratory setting, there is no start signal here for the trial so the emergence of a coalition is an interaction of bottom up sensory signal and implicit top down goal of survival. This emergence of adaptive coalition of only relevant brain processes from the unconscious processing is what I am calling “cognitive integration”. One could argue the emergence of this coherent global conscious state endowed the animal with multimodal discrimination of a state that could not be a priory associated with danger. Put differently, the animal is able to make a classification in the multimodal space by creating a coalition of those brain processes that were separated from other non-relevant sensory input at that moment, pointing to the benefit of having a bottleneck for creating a unitary scene. The relevance of any sensory input at that moment is solely based on prior experience and current context. You can imagine what the consequences are in each scenario if the cognitive integration failed.

I will give an overview of prominent models of consciousness and go through their main tenets to the extent that they are relevant and have implications for cognitive integration (not the quality of perception). Then I will discuss the underlying neuroanatomy, experimental findings and modeling works supporting and elaborating those theories. After which, I discuss how findings from visual search literature can be interpreted in this framework. Finally, I will end with suggesting how cognition and consciousness may share the same foundation at least in living organism so an understanding of cognition can go hand-in-hand with an understanding of consciousness.

**Consciousness and Integration**

The scientific study of consciousness has advanced greatly in the past few decades to the point that we do have a general understanding for how brain dynamics could give rise to our phenomenal experience. The three most prominent of these models, Global workspace theory (Baars, 1989), Dynamic core hypothesis (Tononi & Edelman, 1998; Edelman & Tononi, 2000) and Information integration theory (IIT) (Tononi 2004; Tononi, 2012) share a common foundational framework that could be described as local-global architecture where they all suppose

1.  The existence of anatomically segregated areas (local) that are functionally specialized (for example responding to color and motion in visual cortex).

2.  Mechanisms of (global) integrations through long-range reentrant (recurrent) connectivity among many segregated areas (for example Thalamo-Cortical circuitry or Corpus Callosum connecting the two brain hemispheres).

In these models, processes that are confined to specialists remain unconscious and processes that get integrated globally determine the content of conscious perception. Direct evidence for this architecture for conscious perception comes from a wide range of experiments including contrastive analysis between conscious and conscious brain processes. Mudrik et al. (2014) reviewed experimental evidence for information integration and associations than can happen without conscious awareness. While these associations have a wide range and could vary in semantic depth, there seems to be a general rule in that while short range, low level spatiotemporal integration is possible without awareness, long range high level semantic or multisensory integration requires conscious processing. While these studies shed light on different aspect of conscious/unconscious content dissociation, they should be understood within the larger framework of brain function and dynamics.

**Dynamic Core Hypothesis**

Conscious scenes are at once integrated and differentiated from every other experience we could be having. Dynamic Core hypothesis claims that conscious experience is the process of forming strongly coupled distributed network that is integrating many different sources with the activity segregated from the rest of the brain and creating the so called “Dynamic Core” (Tononi & Edelman, 1998; Edelman & Tononi, 2000).

This hypothesis is an extension of Theory of Neuronal Group Selection (TNGS) also known as Neural Darwinism (Edelman, 1987; Edelman, 1992). According to TNGS, the brain works as a selectional system much like the natural selection but in the somatic time (lifetime). The idea for this proposal came from Edelman’s work in Immunology where Edelman et al. (1969) showed that the reason the immune system can recognize foreign molecule (even the ones that never existed) is because it follows the principle of selectional systems; variations (many variant antibodies), selection and differential amplifications.

Edelman applied those selectional principles to understanding development and dynamics of the nervous system and proposed TNGS with three main tenets (figure 1); developmental selection, experiential selection and reentry, and one constraint; value system.

Neuronal group is a strongly interconnected group of neurons, ranging in size from 100 to 1000 neurons that form the units of selectin. Perin et al. (2011) showed the existence of these neural groups in the cortex forming the so called primary repertoire. Izhikevish et al. (2004) showed that neuronal groups could emerge under various conditions from the interplay of spike time dependent plasticity (Markram et al. 1997) and axonal conduction delays in biological models of spiking neural networks (Izhikevich, 2003; Izhikevich, 2007). The groups were called “Polychromous Neuronal Groups” (PNG) because the neurons do not fire all at once (“synchronous”) but exciting few of them would lead to characteristic spatiotemporal firing pattern in the rest of the group (fig 2). The robustness and generality of this effect later led to suggestions that PNGs showing a reproducible spatiotemporal pattern of neural firing may be the basis for memory (Izhikevich, 2006; Abeles, 2009 on Synfire chains).

Reentry is the bidirectional exchange of signal among anatomically segregated areas in the brain (similar mechanism has been advocated for as “Adaptive Resonances Theory” (ART) by Grossberg, 2013). Reentrant mapping does not carry the feedback signal but mediates the dynamic coordination by differential amplification of the synapses between selected neural groups to create a coherent output (Sporns et al., 1991).

To explore the role of reentry in conscious perception Tononi et al. (1998) used binocular rivalry paradigm where they showed two incongruent stimuli through each eye. The stimuli were red horizontal gratings and blue vertical gratings flickering at different tag frequencies. The subject would be conscious of only one of these stimuli at any time and the perception involuntarily switches between the two conscious percepts. MEG recording revealed that with conscious perception a distributed network of processes synchronized to the same frequency of the stimulus. But more interesting was that also a weaker, less distributed response was present with the frequency of the unconscious stimulus competing for access to the global core.

Any selectional system has its value constraint (survival in natural selection) and neuromodulator systems (figure 3) provide these constraints in the nervous system. With diffuse projections, value systems can affect the excitability and change in synaptic efficacy of the connections in distributed areas of the brain and can modulate the selection of neuronal groups that form the dynamic core so that an adaptive behavior can be generated. In both scenarios 1 and 2, value systems inserted their influence by modulating the formation of a new coalition. Put differently, the coalition that ended up taking control of the global core was the one that predicted/was associated with values important to animals’ goals (not always clear what the goals are). The dopaminergic system has been studied extensively in the reinforcement learning literature following the findings that it might signal the prediction error or change of rewards before action (Schultz et al. 1997). This simple account has been challenged recently when it was shown that the density of dopamine ramps up during a sequence of actions toward a goal (Niv, 2013). The study of reinforcement learning in neuroscience has been mostly dominated by algorithmic methods (Sutton & Barto, 1998; Dayan & Balleine, 2002) with very few minimally successful attempts to implement them in spiking neural networks (Izhikevich, 2007; Urbanczik & Senn, 2009). A great deal of work remains to be done in this field.

In a series of modeling and robotics work for over two decades, Edelman and colleagues have tested different aspects of TNGS (Sporns et al., 1991; Tononi et al., 1992; Seth et al., 2004; and Krichmar et al., 2005). Figure 4a shows the architecture of a simulated brain (automaton) including three parallel streams of visual processing connected with reentrant pathways, eye-movement motor output and an innate value system. The automaton starts by making random eye-movements but when it fixates on a designated object (a red cross in this case) the value system releases “simulated neuromodulators” and as a result those reentrant pathways that led to that behavior get selected and potentiated (conditioning). Figure 4b shows the conditioned automaton when faced with a novel visual input, different coalitions of neuronal groups each representing an object start forming and because the red cross was associated with reward that coalition is strongest and it will gain control to the limited motor output leading to adaptive behavior in this case saccading toward the red cross to get the reward (Friston et al., 1994).

After conditioning, the non-topographic activity in the IT (inferior-temporal cortex) can be used to decode what the automaton is fixating at but the active neurons should not necessarily be thought as a representation of the cross or square because they are not bound to specific locations in the map. This categorical learning emerged through reentrant interaction from behavior under the constraints of the value system.

**Degeneracy and Non-representational Memory**

Introduced to neuroscience research by Edelman & Gally (1999; 2001) degeneracy in biological systems refers to the property that many different circuits can give rise to the same function (Friston & Price, 2003). It exists from genetic code all the way to the motor behavior and is an essential part of any selectional system. In the immune system, this means that many antibody molecules can bind to any one foreign molecule. While this provides redundancy and robustness, it is also essential in the nervous system by allowing a large number of associations to be made without catastrophic forgetting (Grossberg, 2013).

According to TNGS and DCH, memory is a system property. The synaptic efficacy changes are essential but they do not equate memory. The existence of distributed memory traces influences the dynamics of the system through reentry in the way that recreates the mental (image of a recalled memory) or physical action (Edelman & Tononi, 2000). So this means that “every act of memory (recall) is in part an act of imagination” because new traces are created every time, and repeated acts of recall, perception or action can rely on completely degenerate circuits. This is consistent with the recent emergent view of the brain as a dynamical computational system rather that a logical computer with fixed memory (Freeman and Kozma, 2010; Sussillo & Abbott, 2009) Hassabis et al. (2007) showed that amnesic patients with hippocampal damage who had difficulty recalling episodic memories were also unable to richly imagine new scenarios suggesting another link between memory and imagination.

**Metastability**

A healthy brain constantly transitions from one conscious episode to another. This means that there is a lifetime to any dynamic core formation which makes them so called “metastable” (Friston, 1997; Friston, 2000; Rabinovich et al., 2008; Tognoli & Kelso, 2014). While the neuronal groups transiently couple, small perturbation can influence the state of the core completely, suggesting the metastable and critical nature of the core (Werner, 2007). It has been shown that disruption of this metastable dynamics accompanies traumatic brain injury (Hellyer et al., 2015).

**Habits**

If a task is practiced to the point that is mostly proceduralized then it happens without conscious perception. According to the dynamic core hypotheses, this is the direct consequence of the diminishing need for the information that is brought to bear for deciding what to do next. To use a metaphor, during the slow process of learning, a “task force” is trained, but when the task is proceduralized, the task force is automatically called upon by a combination of internal dynamics and bottom up sensory inputs without direct interference of distributed network (so the process remains unconscious).

**Information Integration Theory of consciousness**:

IIT relies on the main tenets of the dynamic core hypothesis, but it also adds a quantification of how much information is generated when segregated areas form a coherent coalition above and beyond the sum of information generated by segregated areas separately (called “Phi”). High values of Phi correspond to conscious alert states and low values correspond to states of dreamless sleep, coma, anesthesia, seizure or completely unconscious (Tononi 2004; Tononi, 2012).

**Global Workspace Theory**

First proposed as a cognitive theory of consciousness by Baars (1988), the Global Workspace Theory (GWT) purports to harnesses the massively parallel architecture of the brain. In this framework, the parallel specialists compete and cooperate for access to the global workspace and when granted access they take control of the core, deposit their information and broadcast their message to other specialists (figure 5). The processing confined to specialists remains unconscious and only information that is broadcasted is perceived consciously. The global workspace goes through a serial procession of states that are the result of integration and competitions between many parallel computations so it can be thought of as a locus of broadcast, a place for competition among different coalitions of specialists, and a medium for communication.

Global Neuronal Workspace (figure 6) was proposed by Dehaene and colleagues (1998; 2005; 2006) as a neural model of GWT. In a series of experiments, they used masking paradigm to contrast the brain activity when the subject became conscious of a flashed number or word (figure 7). They consistently observed an all-or-none response in behavior and recordings in that when the subject was not conscious of the number, the activity remained localized and faded immediately in the visual areas but with conscious reporting came an “ignition” of activity lighting up and spreading across both hemispheres of the brain. Their implementation of global neuronal workspace was able to account for these effects contrasting faint and strong bottom up inputs. The model was also shown to account for attentional blink in RSVP (Rapid Serial Visual Presentation, Marois & Chun, 2004) tasks where the recognition of the first target (allocated attention) hinders the recognition of the target that comes right after (refractory period would map unto the duration of broadcast and competition \~ 180 ms).

GWT, in its most recent version, could be understood as a special case of dynamic core hypothesis; the theory has an extra claim that there is a directional flow of information from the current (winning and broadcasting) coalition to next forming coalitions (Baars, 2013).

**Binding problem, Selective Attention and Global Core**

The binding problem has a long history in psychology and phenomenology of perception. Simply put, if different features of a sensory stimuli (or an imagined one) are processed (discriminated) by different, spatially distributed areas of the brain, how can we perceive the stimulus as a coherent whole and how can that guide our behavior? For the special case of visual feature binding, Treisman and colleagues (1980; 1990) offered “Feature integration theory” according to which, the brain binds the features of the stimulus coming from the same spatial location. But, in order for the binding to occur, one has to selectively attend to that location either overtly or covertly. Early support for this theory came from experiments showing subjects cannot recognize objects pre-attentively and that illusory conjunctions were made for the stimuli in the periphery (wrong assignment of color or orientation). All were explained by supposing that only very simple features are processed and generated pre-attentively and the features of objects in the periphery are not binded.

Further evidences were from visual search literature where the extent of pre-attentive processing has been discussed and explored extensively. The main measure in this field is the search efficiency (from coarse measures such as response time to more detailed eye-movement measures such as initial saccade direction) for which a main factor is search guidance. If eye-movements are not guided toward the target, the fixations will be randomly deployed and the subject will, on average, look at half of the items in the display (set-size) to find the target. But if the search is guided the task would be much more efficient (a shallower slope of reaction time as a function of set-size). Therefore, the question of pre-attentive processing is explored through studying what non-simple features of the target can guide attention because when the target is not being fixated at a moment, it is processed pre-attentively and the more complex the guiding features, the more complex the possible integration. Since pre-attentive processes take place in parallel and are very fast, the more complex integration leads to more efficient search for complex objects.

Since Treisman’s seminal work (1980), feature maps have been a prominent element in models of visual search and selective attention. These topographic maps of color, motion, orientation and etc. are located in different areas of the brain and are created pre-attentively and again according to feature integration theory selective attention to an area in this map is needed for the features of that object to bind together. In this framework only simple features can guide eye-movement when the object share those features with the target making that feature map to have a higher value in that location. Therefore the model correctly predicted that conjunction search (search for a red vertical bar) takes more time than searching for a single feature target (red bar or vertical bar). But the slopes of reaction times, as a function of set-size, are much shallower than predicted by this model, also triple conjunction (short red vertical) is in some cases faster than double conjunction. Based on these evidences, Wolfe and colleagues (1989; 1994) proposed the “Guided search” model extending the main framework of Feature Integration theory. In Guided search, any combination of basic features can guide attention in that subjects would attend to a stimulus that has both red color and vertical orientation through combining basic feature maps into an activation map (also called a “Master map” or “Priority map”). Still, the subjects would have to visit the peak activities on this map to bind the features together and either reject or accept it as target. Wolfe and Horowitz (2004) later documented basic features that contribute to the building of this activation map but their account is only applicable to stimuli with simple feature dimensions such as color, orientation etc. Recent studies (from Zelinsky’s group, 2013) have shown that attention is guided to complex real world stimuli and even to categorically defined ones and whether it is possible to explain these findings in terms of biasing different basic feature map is yet to be explored.

There still is an important serial stage in the Guided search model that puts constraints on search efficiency, requiring 40 ms per item processing time to fit the data, which is significantly shorter than the time that has been experimentally recorded (around 100 ms) to take to recognize an object (Vanrullen & Thorpe 2001; Johnson & Olshausen, 2003). This means either few objects are processed in parallel or, as Wolfe (2003) argued, with each fixation features of few objects are binded through fast covert shifts of attention and entered into a processing cue (as in a “carwash” it takes some time to wash one car but a new car does not have to wait for the current one to exit before entering). While most studies show covert and overt attentions are fundamentally linked with covert attention often preceding overt attention, Wolfe’s argument hinges on possible dissociations between them (Wright & Ward, 2008). In other words, pre-attentive and peripheral are not always the same and it is possible to make judgments on or even recognize a big enough covertly attended object while maintaining fixation at other place on the display. On the other hand, studies of attentional blindness (the invisible gorilla) or change blindness are showing that subjects are virtually blind to changes that are happening in the periphery (Simons and Levin, 1997) casting doubt on a covert “car wash” mechanism.

With recent experimental and neurophysiological findings on search guidance to complex objects and the recent resurge of feedforward visual processing models (supposedly of ‘what’ pathways) the more accepted account is that the object categories are processed pre-attentively. Studies have shown that category selective response is present in areas V4 and IT in about 100 ms after stimulus onset (Thorpe et al., 1996; Riesenhuber, & Poggio, 1999; Bichot et al., 2005; Hung et al., 2005; DiCarlo et al., 2012). Experimental findings in our lab certainly points toward a fast parallel search mechanism to model which we needed to create priority maps quantifying the distance of each object from classification boundary of target category in parallel for all objects (Zelinsky et al. 2013). Connecting this to the point on habit formation, the process of learning the regularities in visual categories was a slow attentive process, possibly through reentrant interaction, that leads to feedforward proceduralized pre-attentive selection of categories in the adult brain.

As discussed earlier, figure 4 shows an automaton that was conditioned to search for red crosses so there was no target cueing in that task. When initially fixated at the center, different coalitions from the three objects in the display started forming and through competition (lateral inhibition) for Global core, the strongest one emerged and because it is tagging a location (through topographic maps) it drives the eye-movement to itself. In this framework, objects are processed in parallel and if the coalition they form are strong enough they will guide the eye-movement. In other words, the attention is guided because of peripheral recognition of the valued stimulus (Hikosaka et al., 2014). A signature of this parallel processing can be observed in search behavior where a subject looks away from the target but then immediately looks back toward the target; because the formation of the coalition with that target is occurring pre-attentively and it took some time for the target to win the competition. To speculate how this framework would account for search guidance to a cued target, it would be in the form of facilitating the formation target coalition. When a cue (exemplar or categorical) is imagined prior to search display onset, it will activate distributed traces in the brain and (GWT can add an element of broadcast here that can strongly modulate early visual areas) therefore can facilitate the creation of coalition that share features with the target.

One could ask: Where is the target map in this simulated brain? The answer is everywhere\! Not surprisingly, researchers have found priority maps everywhere in the brain from most posterior parts (V1; Li, 2002) to parietal (Bisley & Goldberg, 2010) and frontal areas (Schall & Hanes, 1993) to brain stem and Superior Colliculus (Mcpeek & Keller, 2004; Krauzlis et al., 2004; Fecteau & Munoz, 2006). As shown in Figure 4b, the dynamic core is distributed across the entire brain and the neurons that are part of the wining coalition will show increased activity compared to other neurons and their own baseline, it is in fact the global mapping of all these local maps that forms the dynamic core. The neuronal groups within the collations that do no win would also show higher than baseline activity depending on the strength of the coalition.

So how about the temporal relationship between those maps? This brings us to the second well known offer to solve the binding problem, by Singer and colleagues (1989) so called “binding by synchrony”. They proposed that different areas of the brain selective for a specific stimuli, synchronize their response into coherent oscillations. Original evidence for this came from works of Gray et al. (1989) where they showed different areas of the cat visual cortex synchronized their response within gamma frequency in response to an oriented bar. The original form of this theory has faced a great deal of criticism (Shadlen & Monshon, 1999) in that it is not clear how different areas can communicate or do computations if they are firing at complete synchrony (Singer, 2007).

Desimone, Fries and colleagues (2001; 2005; 2009) showed that when monkeys covertly attend to a spatial location, not only does the firing rate of neurons in V4 with receptive fields on the attended object increase (at the expense of dampening the activity of distractors) but also the spikes synchronize to the local field potential recorded from nearby neurons. Based on this evidence, they proposed the temporal synchrony mechanism of selective attention; stating that attention modulates the temporal structure of the spike trains which results in higher firing rates. The main idea is that if some of the neurons sending signal to a target neuron, synchronize their activity so that they reach that neuron in close proximity in time, then they can have a greater impact. Womelsdorf et al. (2006) showed that the amount of coherence was predictive of reaction time to respond to color change in attended stimuli, further supporting the cognitive importance of local coherence in selective attention.

Bichot et al. (2005) extended this methodology to a visual search experiment where monkeys performed conjunction (color and shape) search. During search, when the target was in the receptive field of the neurons in V4, it had a higher firing rate and higher coherence to local field potential within gamma range even when the monkey did not saccade to the target. Combined recording of FEF and V4 showed that FEF also exhibits the gamma band coherence that came later and with half a cycle phase difference from area V4. What was interesting about the finding was that even in FEF they found the color selectivity and coherency for the target in the receptive field before the saccade but when the monkey did not eventually saccade to that location the activity dampened. They hypothesized that FEF biases the early maps in V4 based on a target template and selects one location from the candidates to be attended to (one that makes the content of conscious scene, awareness and potentially could be recalled after the task). The half a cycle phase different in coherence between FEF and V4 makes sense when taking into account the axonal delays between these areas. FEF would have the highest influence on V4 if the neurons within FEF synchronize earlier in time so that their signal reaches V4 at V4’s highest excitability period.

These finding gave rise to a new related theory of binding; “Communication through Coherences”. A coalition of neuronal groups is distributed across brain hemispheres on multiple hierarchical levels, the formation of which depends on efficient signaling and coordination between the nodes. The theory of communication through coherence (Fries, 2005; Fries, 2009; Womelsdorf et al., 2007) suggests that the rhythms in the brain play a major role because the neuronal groups can effectively communicate when they are at or near peak excitability on the wave (or coexisting waves) they are riding on (figure 8a).

The previously mentioned studies show that distributed networks of posterior/parietal to frontal areas are created for multiple objects and are competing for global access and affecting behavior (a similar account is given by Hamker, 2005 as “Reentry Hypothesis”). Their competition is modulated by their behavioral relevance (similarity to the target in the visual search task) and the reentrant connectivity that partially creates the oscillations can block out other sensory inputs and segregate the winning coalition. The near synchronous firing of the coalition mediated by global workspace (figure 8b) makes them transiently stable and strong enough to affect the downstream neurons. The value systems also play an important role by widely influencing the excitability of different circuits and modulating the rhythms.

**Does the brain instantiate the GW architecture?**

The answer should come from the new field of connectomics (Sporns et al., 2005) studying the large scale connectivity in the brain. Using diffusion tensor imaging and graph theoretic techniques, Sporns and Colleagues (2005; 2008) have shown that the brain shows the properties of small-world networks (figure 9a), where any node in the network can access any other node by only going through very few links (compare that to fig 8a without the green connections). Another large scale connectivity property is modularity, in that brain areas that are close together are going to form a cluster (figures 9b and 10a) and this modular connectivity is hierarchical (Modha & Singh, 2010 ).

Figures 9b and 9c show two different modular small world networks but the network in figure 9c has connector hubs, a group of neurons that play an important role in communications between different clusters, also known as “rich club” nodes (figure 10b). Harriger et al. (2012) has shown that indeed brain network has rich club nodes that attract and disperse a large amount of signals.

Harriger et al. (2012) and Shanahan (2012) hypothesized that the existence of rich-club organization in the hierarchically modular network has significant implications for the dynamic functioning of the brain. Because of their connectivity, large traffic of signals fan into the rich club nodes and fan out of them to the rest of the network making them a great candidate for global workspace. Signals reaching the rich club nodes (connective core) will potentially have access to large number of areas (“any to many signaling”) and can cause the “ignition” and reverberation of activity as observed by Deheane and colleagues (1998; 2005).

**Dynamic Complexity**

Complexity lies between fully random and fully structured organizations. Neural complexity measures the extent to which the dynamics of a neural system are both integrated and differentiated (Tononi et al., 1994). Using different measures of complexity, Sporns et al. (2000) showed that small world connectivity gives rise to a more complex (brain-like) profile of activity compared to either completely random or completely structured networks (figure 11). Casual density is another way of quantifying the integration and differentiation in a dynamical system (Seth et al., 2006; Seth et al., 2011). It measures the causal interactivity among different areas from applying time-series Granger causality (Seth, 2007) analysis and has been shown to correlate well with the consciousness level (high values for alert conscious state and low values for unconscious and anesthesia states). Shanahan (2008a) explored the dynamic complexity of spiking networks with small-world connectivity. The networks were constructed by applying a probabilistic rewiring to first separated clusters. The quantifications of complexity using causal density and information integration supports the result that small world network show greater complexity in their neural firing patterns but the effects were present for only a very narrow settings of clustering parameters.

Shanahan (2008b) offered a spiking neuron model of cortical competition and broadcast. In this model, the workspace is comprised of five strongly interconnected nodes capable of reverberating and maintaining spatiotemporal patterns of activity (figure 12). Three cortical columns connected to these workspace nodes, forming modular network, received sensory input and each reentranly exchanged signals with a workspace node and other columns within the cluster. The cortical columns were separately trained to output a pattern of firing upon receiving a certain input. So for a given input, one or more cortical columns would respond to that pattern and compete to access to the workspace; if they won, their output signal will take control of the workspace and reverberate and broadcast to the rest of the network where in turn a new pattern may emerge through lateral inhibition and top-down influence of broadcast (figure 12b shows the state to state transiting of a workspace node). While being small in size and narrow in scope, this model does capture important properties of GWT and guides the future simulations.

While a convincing model of coalition or dynamic core formation is still missing, some works have shown how metastable states of short term synchronization can emerge in networks of coupled oscillators (Shanahan, 2010a; Wildie & Shanahan, 2012) where a subset of nodes synchronize while being desynchronized from the rest of the system (Kuramoto & Battogtokh , 2002; Abrams & Strogatz, 2004; Hong & Strogatz, 2011). Cabrel et al. (2011) successfully used this small-world network of coupled oscillators to predict the correlated activity between brain areas in the resting state FMRI, modeling the so called “functional connectivity” arising from the structural connectivity.

**Coalition Formation and Cognitive Integration**

Adaptive behavior requires cognitive integration across many domains. When faced with a novel situation, a new coalition of brain processes (figure 13) will emerge partly under the influence of internal dynamics and partly due to external input. This coalition will be distributed across the brain including arbitrary combination of sensory, motor, conceptual and perceptual memory systems (Bressler & Menon, 2001).

The brain dynamically maintains a large variable repertoire of metastable collations that could be experientially selected from to create not only the already experienced collations and actions but ones that has never existed or imagined. This repertoire of variability is not noise but a must in the selectional system without which the system would fail to bring together the relevant information.

I reviewed here a body of theoretical, computational and experimental works to support the claim that understanding cognition may go hand-in-hand with understanding consciousness; that metastable dynamics, complexity and selectional features are the shared underlying foundation. This framework seems to be necessary for any cognitive system hoping to transcend specialized modules and specific tasks to general adaptive behavior.
